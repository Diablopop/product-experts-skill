# Product Expert Panel: Strategic stability when leadership keeps changing direction

**Date:** 2026-02-15
**Your Question:**
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

---

## Executive Summary

All five experts converge on a core diagnosis: the quarterly priority shifts are not a planning failure or a personality problem -- they are a symptom of a missing strategic filter. The CEO is not being erratic for the sake of it; they lack a durable framework (whether a strategic narrative, a mission-to-OKR stack, or a written strategy document) to evaluate new ideas against. Without that filter, every market signal, board conversation, or competitor move becomes the new top priority because there is no principled way to say no.

The experts diverge on *where to start fixing this*, and that divergence is useful. Wes Kao frames it as a managing-up problem and recommends starting with communication rhythms (weekly updates, trade-off framing). Andy Raskin says the company needs a strategic narrative and the PM should be the catalyst. Christina Wodtke would start with celebrations and cadence rituals to rebuild morale while pushing the CEO to articulate the mission/strategy/OKR hierarchy. Shreyas Doshi says the PM should take the high-agency move of writing the strategy document themselves. Nickey Skarstad emphasizes co-creating the strategy with both the CEO and the team so everyone has ownership.

The combined advice suggests a sequenced approach: (1) start immediately with communication and morale-building tactics, (2) build the strategic artifact (narrative, strategy doc, or pyramid), (3) use trade-offs and first principles to manage incoming priority shifts, and (4) pilot sustained focus with one team to build evidence that stability produces better outcomes.

## Key Takeaways & Action Items

1. **Build the strategic filter the CEO is missing**
   - Recommended by: Shreyas Doshi, Andy Raskin, Christina Wodtke, Nickey Skarstad
   - How to apply: Write a one-page document that articulates the company's mission, yearly strategy, and how quarterly priorities connect to it. Use Raskin's five-piece narrative framework or Wodtke's mission/strategy/OKR stack. Share it with the CEO for input and co-ownership. Once it exists, every new priority gets evaluated against it: "How does this advance our strategy?"

2. **Surface trade-offs instead of pushing back**
   - Recommended by: Wes Kao, Shreyas Doshi, Nickey Skarstad
   - How to apply: When the CEO introduces a new priority, respond with: "We can prioritize this. That means X and Y get delayed by Z weeks. Which do you prefer?" This forces the CEO to confront the real cost and positions Andrew as a partner, not a blocker. Use Nickey's one-way/two-way door framework to classify requests and propose lightweight experiments for smaller asks.

3. **Start a communication rhythm immediately**
   - Recommended by: Wes Kao, Christina Wodtke, Nickey Skarstad
   - How to apply: Send a short weekly "state of the roadmap" update to the CEO (current priorities, trade-offs, upcoming decisions). Implement Monday commitments and Friday celebrations with the team. Record short Loom updates after leadership decisions. This reduces CEO surprise-interventions and rebuilds team morale in parallel.

4. **Pilot sustained focus with one team and measure the difference**
   - Recommended by: Christina Wodtke, Nickey Skarstad
   - How to apply: Pick the highest-performing team, give them one clear objective for the full quarter, and protect them from mid-quarter pivots. At quarter-end, compare their results to a team that absorbed a pivot. Grade OKRs even if priorities changed mid-quarter to build a factual record of the cost of instability.

5. **Investigate what is actually driving the CEO's shifting priorities**
   - Recommended by: Wes Kao, Andy Raskin, Shreyas Doshi
   - How to apply: Track the last several priority shifts and look for a pattern -- board pressure, competitive anxiety, a specific metric, customer escalations. Use Wes's "eyes light up" principle and Raskin's customer interview approach. Once the pattern is clear, proactively address the underlying concern within the existing roadmap, reducing the chance the CEO feels the need to override.
