# Advice from Shreyas Doshi

## Expert Background
Shreyas Doshi is a product management expert who has worked as both an IC and manager at Yahoo, Google, Twitter, and Stripe. He is known for sharing consistent, contrarian insights on building products and the role of product management, always rooted in real-world experience at high-growth companies.

## Response to Your Question
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

## Key Insights

1. **The quarterly shifting is almost certainly a strategy problem, not an execution problem.** Shreyas makes a core argument that most execution problems are actually strategy problems in disguise. When a CEO keeps changing direction every quarter, the real issue is the absence of a clear, durable strategy. The shifting priorities are a symptom. The rotating "top priorities" reveal that no one has done the harder work of defining a strategy that can withstand quarter-to-quarter noise. Solving this at the execution level (better roadmap processes, more alignment meetings) will produce bandaids that keep falling off.

2. **Bandaids falling off is the diagnostic signal.** If the team has tried alignment exercises, re-prioritization frameworks, or new planning processes and the problem keeps recurring, that is Shreyas's signature indicator that the root cause is upstream -- either a missing strategy, a culture problem, or both. Stop treating it as a planning problem and name it as a strategy problem.

3. **The CEO may be operating at a different "level of product work" than the team.** Shreyas's framework of impact, execution, and optics explains why leadership and teams talk past each other. The CEO is likely reacting to new impact-level signals (a competitor move, a board conversation, a big customer request) and translating them directly into execution-level mandates without a strategy layer in between to absorb and filter those signals. The team, meanwhile, is operating at the execution level and getting whiplash.

4. **Opportunity cost thinking can reframe the conversation with the CEO.** Rather than arguing "we should finish what we started" (which sounds like resistance), Shreyas would frame the conversation around opportunity cost: "By switching to this new priority, here is what we are giving up, and here is the cost of the context switch itself." This moves the discussion from emotional ("we're frustrated") to analytical ("here is the tradeoff").

5. **High agency is the PM's path through this.** Shreyas defines high agency as finding a way to get what you want without waiting for conditions to be perfect or blaming circumstances. Waiting for the CEO to stop changing direction is low agency. The high-agency move is to take ownership of the strategy gap yourself and fill it, even if "strategy" is not technically in the PM's job description at this company.

## Actionable Advice

- **Name the problem accurately.** In your next planning cycle or one-on-one with the CEO, frame the issue directly: "We don't have a strategy problem because we lack ideas. We have a strategy problem because we lack a durable strategy that tells us what to say no to. Every quarter, a new signal arrives and we pivot because there is no strategic filter to evaluate it against." Shreyas would say this is the L task (leverage task) that the PM knows they need to do but keeps avoiding because it is hard and uncomfortable.

- **Run a pre-mortem on the current quarter's priority.** Shreyas's pre-mortem framework is directly applicable here. Gather the team at the start of the quarter and prompt: "Imagine it is 90 days from now and this new top priority has failed. What went wrong?" The tigers, paper tigers, and elephants that surface will almost certainly include "we got redirected again mid-quarter" or "we did not have enough time to see results before the next pivot." This gives the team psychological safety to voice concerns, and it gives the PM a documented artifact to bring to the CEO showing the team's real assessment of risk.

- **Propose a resource allocation framework to the CEO.** Shreyas used a 60/30/10 split (incrementals, big new initiatives, stability/infrastructure) to give teams planning clarity. Propose something similar to the CEO: "Let's commit 70% of our capacity to the agreed strategic priority and protect it for the full quarter. The remaining 30% can absorb new requests, experiments, and CEO-initiated explorations." This gives the CEO a legitimate channel for new ideas without blowing up the core roadmap.

- **Create the strategy document yourself.** Do not wait for the CEO to write a strategy. Write a one-page strategy that synthesizes what you understand about the company's direction, the market, and the customer. Share it with the CEO for feedback. Shreyas observed at Stripe that the best PMs do not wait for perfect conditions -- they take ownership and create the artifact that is missing. This is the high-agency move.

- **Use the vocabulary to depersonalize the conflict.** Shreyas repeatedly emphasizes that shared vocabulary (tigers/paper tigers/elephants, L/N/O tasks, impact/execution/optics) lets teams talk about problems objectively rather than pointing fingers. Introduce the "strategy vs. execution problem" framing to the CEO: "I think we keep solving this as an execution problem, but it might actually be a strategy problem. Can we spend time clarifying our strategic filter before we plan the next quarter?"

## Relevant Experience

- **At Stripe, Shreyas observed the power of strategy-first planning.** When he took over Stripe Connect, he noticed the team was filling their plate with positive-ROI quick wins. He pushed the team to pursue a bigger, scarier initiative around flexible marketplace payments because customer signals pointed to growing demand. This required resisting the gravitational pull of short-term wins. The parallel to Andrew's situation: the CEO's quarterly pivots are the organizational equivalent of chasing quick wins at the strategic level.

- **At Google, Shreyas experienced the $100 million filter.** Google's ads team had an unwritten rule that opportunities under $100 million were not worth pursuing. This was a strategic filter that prevented exactly the kind of whiplash Andrew is experiencing. The filter was not always right (Google missed some trends because of it), but it provided stability and focus. The lesson: even an imperfect strategic filter is better than no filter at all.

- **Shreyas's own experience with being overwhelmed at Google.** He described his first three years at Google as the most stressful of his career -- endless to-do lists, constant firefighting, no sense of progress. The breakthrough came when he learned to distinguish between leverage tasks and overhead tasks. The same principle applies at the organizational level: a company that treats every new CEO idea as equally urgent is the organizational equivalent of a PM who treats every task as equally important.

- **LNO framework for task prioritization** -- Distinguishing leverage, neutral, and overhead tasks to focus energy on what matters most, relevant to helping the PM identify that fixing the strategy gap is the highest-leverage task available
- **Three levels of product work (impact, execution, optics)** -- Understanding that misalignment between leadership and teams often stems from operating at different levels, which explains why the CEO's impact-level reactions create execution-level chaos
