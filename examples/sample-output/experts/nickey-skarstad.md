# Advice from Nickey Skarstad

## Expert Background
Nickey Skarstad is a Director of Product Management at Duolingo with 12 years of technology experience and 10 years in product roles. She built a legendary reputation across Etsy, Airbnb, and other companies for her expertise in marketplace product strategy, product quality, and getting product-market fit.

## Response to Your Question
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

## Key Insights

1. **Your team needs to co-create the strategy, not just receive it.** Nickey is emphatic that strategy built in a vacuum -- even by a senior leader -- fails to generate buy-in. If the CEO's quarterly pivots land as top-down edicts, the team will never feel ownership. When people help craft the strategy, they become its defenders rather than its victims. This is the single biggest lever Andrew has: pull the team into the strategy-setting process so they have a stake in protecting it.

2. **A clear vision-mission-strategy-objectives pyramid is the antidote to shiny object syndrome.** Nickey uses a top-down pyramid (vision at the top, then mission, strategy, then objectives/OKRs) that forces every near-term decision to connect to a long-term destination. When a CEO throws in a new priority, the pyramid gives Andrew a concrete artifact to point to: "Does this serve the 10-year vision? Where does it fit in our strategy?" Without that artifact, there is no defense against constant reprioritization.

3. **Teams that are deeply bought into a vision are less distractable.** Nickey observes that the real vulnerability to shifting priorities comes not from the team level, but from leadership that is not in the weeds day-to-day. When the team has internalized the strategy, they naturally filter noise. The problem Andrew describes -- morale dropping and belief eroding -- signals the team was never given a strategy robust enough to hold onto.

4. **Use first principles alignment as an early gate to prevent wasted work.** Nickey describes getting alignment on first principles (what matters, what the team is solving for, and why) as "almost the most important" approval gate. When Andrew can get the CEO to agree on first principles before the quarter begins, subsequent "new ideas" can be evaluated against those principles rather than treated as blank-slate pivots.

5. **Distinguish one-way door decisions from two-way door decisions.** Not every CEO request demands a full roadmap overhaul. Nickey recommends classifying decisions by reversibility. Many new CEO priorities may actually be two-way door experiments that the team can run without derailing the core roadmap. Framing them this way protects the long-term plan while still being responsive to leadership.

## Actionable Advice

- **Build the pyramid and make it visible.** Write down a clear vision (10-year horizon), mission, strategy, and quarterly objectives. Share it broadly. Use it in every planning discussion. When the CEO introduces a new priority, pull up the pyramid and ask explicitly: where does this fit? This is not about saying no -- it is about forcing prioritization against an agreed-upon framework.

- **Bring the CEO into the strategy process early and directly.** Nickey stresses that PMs who do not talk to leadership and get organizational context end up building strategy in a vacuum, only to have it overwritten later. Andrew should proactively schedule time with the CEO to align on the long-term vision and first principles before quarterly planning begins. If the CEO helped shape the strategy, they are less likely to blow it up.

- **Close the feedback loop fast.** After leadership reviews or decisions, Nickey records a short Loom video and posts it in Slack the same day. Andrew should adopt a similar practice: whenever a priority decision is made (or changed), communicate the reasoning immediately and transparently. This prevents the corrosive effect of teams hearing about changes through the grapevine and feeling blindsided.

- **Run a weekly strategy check-in.** Nickey holds a recurring team meeting where the team explicitly revisits: What is our strategy? How are our current goals laddering up? Are we on track? This constant reconnection to the plan gives the team a psychological anchor. It also creates a natural forum to surface CEO requests and evaluate them together rather than reacting in panic.

- **Use first principles as a shield.** Document and get leadership sign-off on the first principles for the quarter. When a new priority arrives, evaluate it against those principles. Nickey's language for this is direct: "Yes, however, it's not in service of this long-term plan, so we don't want to do that right now because we don't think it's the best use of our time." That framing is not insubordination -- it is disciplined product management.

- **Classify new CEO requests using the one-way/two-way door framework.** Some requests can be run as lightweight experiments (two-way doors) without displacing the roadmap. Others genuinely require deep investment (one-way doors) and deserve the full debate. Offering this classification to the CEO shows strategic maturity and gives them a way to get curiosity satisfied without blowing up the plan.

## Relevant Experience

- **At Airbnb Experiences**, Nickey joined a nascent product that needed its own space and process to succeed. The team was given a GM structure with autonomy precisely so they could maintain strategic focus without being pulled into the priorities of the broader company. This structural autonomy was essential -- Nickey believes Experiences "never would have succeeded" inside a traditional functional structure where competing priorities from the larger organization could override their focus. This is a direct parallel to Andrew's situation: the team needs some form of structural or process-based protection from constant reprioritization.

- **At Etsy**, Nickey's team initially optimized the seller onboarding flow for pure growth (open as many shops as possible), then realized they had tanked downstream quality metrics. They had to step back, define what actually mattered (sellers getting a first sale within seven days), and realign the entire team around that balancing metric. The lesson: having the right long-term metric -- one that balances growth with quality -- gives the team a stable anchor that survives quarter-to-quarter priority shifts. Andrew could use a similar approach: define a durable north star metric that transcends any single quarter's priority.

- **At Duolingo**, Nickey described her quarterly OKR process as one that starts in the third month of the current quarter, involves the team in drafting what the next quarter should look like, goes up for leadership approval, and then is communicated back quickly. This disciplined cadence creates a rhythm the team can rely on. The instability Andrew describes often comes from the absence of such a rhythm.
