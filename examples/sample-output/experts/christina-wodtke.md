# Advice from Christina Wodtke

## Expert Background
Christina Wodtke is a multi-time author, Stanford lecturer, and former product leader at LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, and Yahoo who specializes in OKR processes, product development, and organizational focus. She is the author of *Radical Focus*, the definitive practitioner's guide to implementing OKRs.

## Response to Your Question
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

## Key Insights

1. **The CEO's behavior is a strategy problem, not an OKR problem.** Wodtke is emphatic that OKRs are "a vitamin, not a medicine." If the CEO has no stable strategy, layering OKRs on top will just expose the dysfunction. The root issue is that the company lacks a durable mission and strategy that sits *above* quarterly priorities. Without that anchor, every shiny object becomes the new top priority because there is no shared test for what matters.

2. **Constantly shifting priorities is a symptom of missing "why."** Wodtke stresses that companies set vague missions and then forget them. If the CEO cannot articulate why this quarter's priority connects to a five-year mission and a yearly strategy, then the priority is arbitrary. A PM can intervene by forcing the conversation backward: *Why this? How does it connect to the mission? What does the year look like?* That framework gives both the CEO and teams a reason to say no to distractions.

3. **Speed of change is the enemy of learning.** Wodtke's entire OKR cadence is built on a learning cycle: commit on Monday, celebrate on Friday, grade at quarter-end, retrospect, and then set the next quarter. If the CEO blows up the OKR mid-quarter, the team never completes the cycle and never learns what works. The company gets dumber over time, not smarter. That is likely what the morale problem actually is: people feel like their work is wasted because there is no feedback loop.

4. **Psychological safety collapses when direction keeps changing.** Wodtke connects OKR dysfunction directly to leadership trust. If people keep bringing small decisions up to the CEO or freezing when priorities shift, it is because the environment is not safe enough for teams to own their own direction. The CEO may be the one creating that dynamic by constantly re-steering.

5. **You do not need to fix the whole company to start.** Wodtke consistently recommends starting small. A PM can pilot stability with one team, prove the value of sustained focus over a full quarter, and use those results to build credibility upward.

## Actionable Advice

- **Force the strategic stack into existence.** Before next quarter's OKR cycle, push the CEO to articulate three things in writing: (1) the company's mission (what does the company exist to do over the next five years?), (2) a yearly strategy (a "strongly held hypothesis about a way to win in the market"), and (3) the quarterly OKR that flows from that strategy. When the CEO proposes a mid-quarter pivot, reference the stack: "How does this connect to our yearly strategy?" This gives the conversation structure instead of making it a power struggle.

- **Use the "half-built strategy" approach.** Wodtke recommends mapping the year's four quarters to rough objectives but only detailing key results for the current quarter. This gives the CEO a visible plan that acknowledges future flexibility while locking the current quarter. Present it as: "Q1 we focus on retention, Q2 we shift to acquisition, Q3 we invest in partnerships, Q4 we scale what worked." The CEO can still influence the sequence, but the existence of a plan makes impulsive shifts more visible and costly.

- **Implement Monday commitments and Friday celebrations immediately.** Even without formal OKRs, start the cadence. Monday: "What are we doing this week to move toward our goal?" Friday: "What was the most awesome thing that happened this week?" Wodtke has seen CEOs report that Friday celebrations alone changed morale before OKRs were even introduced. This gives teams a sense of progress and belonging even in a chaotic environment.

- **Grade OKRs at quarter-end no matter what.** Even if the CEO changed direction mid-quarter, run the retrospective. "We aimed for X, the CEO shifted us to Y in week six, here is what we accomplished on each." This creates a factual record of the cost of pivoting. Over two or three quarters, that record becomes compelling evidence that stability produces better outcomes.

- **Pilot with the best team.** Pick the highest-performing team, give them one clear OKR for the quarter, protect them from mid-quarter pivots, and let them run. At quarter-end, compare their results to a team that absorbed a pivot. Wodtke recommends this approach because high-performing teams will adapt the process to the company's culture and produce visible proof of concept.

- **Use status emails to create transparency.** Institute a short weekly status update for each team: confidence level on key results, what was done last week, what is planned next week. Make these visible across the company. Wodtke found these invaluable for surfacing what is actually getting done versus what the CEO says should be getting done. Transparency makes priority whiplash undeniable.

## Relevant Experience

Wodtke draws heavily on her experience at Zynga, where short OKR status updates with P1/P2/P3 priorities and confidence levels created a company-wide rhythm of accountability. She describes the system where status emails were shared on email lists anyone could subscribe to, enabling cross-functional visibility. This is directly applicable: if Andrew's company had this level of transparency, the CEO would see the downstream cost of mid-quarter pivots in real time.

She also references the "peanut butter memo" from Yahoo, where the company tried to do everything at once, spreading resources thin across dozens of initiatives. This is precisely the pattern Andrew is describing: each new CEO priority does not replace the old one so much as it adds another thin layer of effort across the company. Wodtke's antidote is radical focus: one big rock per quarter, moved decisively.

Her consulting experience with companies that lack strategy is also directly relevant. Wodtke says she has been "really shocked lately" at how many companies have no strategy whatsoever. The CEO introducing a new top priority every quarter is, in Wodtke's framing, a company that has replaced strategy with reaction.

## Related Topics This Expert Covered
- **OKR cadence and rituals** -- Monday commitments, Friday celebrations, weekly status emails, and quarterly grading create a rhythm that resists mid-cycle disruption
- **Mission/vision/strategy hierarchy** -- A five-year mission feeds a yearly strategy feeds quarterly OKRs; without this stack, quarterly priorities float untethered
- **Psychological safety and trust** -- OKR dysfunction is often a symptom of deeper leadership and trust problems; teams need to feel safe to say "this is not working" and to own decisions without escalation
- **Piloting change with high-performing teams** -- Start small, prove value, then expand; never try to fix a broken team or broken leadership with a new process
