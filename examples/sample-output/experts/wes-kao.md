# Advice from Wes Kao

## Expert background
Wes Kao is the co-founder of Maven, a cohort-based learning platform, and previously co-founded the altMBA with Seth Godin. She is recognized as an expert in managing up, stakeholder influence, executive communication, and saying no effectively.

## Response to your question
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

## Key insights

- **This is a managing up problem, not a strategy problem.** The instinct is to try to fix the roadmap or build a better strategy document. But the root issue is that the CEO is making priority shifts without understanding the trade-offs, and no one is surfacing those trade-offs clearly enough. The CEO is not being malicious -- they likely lack visibility into the downstream cost of changing direction every quarter.

- **Talk about trade-offs instead of saying no.** Wes's core framework for saying no applies directly here. Rather than pushing back on the CEO's new priority (which will be perceived as being uncooperative or not a team player), frame every priority change as a trade-off conversation. What gets delayed? What gets cut? What cost does the team absorb? When the CEO sees the full picture, they may self-correct -- or at least make a more informed decision.

- **Proactively keep leadership in the loop so surprises stop flowing in both directions.** Much of the chaos from shifting priorities comes from the CEO not having enough context about what the team is already doing and why. If leadership does not see the current work as high-value, they will keep replacing it with whatever feels urgent this quarter. Wes emphasizes that the most senior, most effective people are the ones who manage up best -- and that starts with proactive communication so the boss is never in a position to be blindsided or to blindside the team.

- **Structure communication so decision-makers can actually engage.** Wes is emphatic that how something is communicated matters as much as what is communicated. If trade-offs, priorities, and recommendations are buried in context or presented as vague concerns, leadership will ignore them. Put the recommendation and the core trade-off at the top; put supporting context below for those who want it.

- **Use the "eyes light up" principle to understand what is actually driving the CEO.** Rather than being frustrated by shifting priorities, study what pattern is behind them. What makes the CEO's eyes light up? Is it revenue growth, competitive threats, board pressure, a new market opportunity? If there is a pattern, Andrew can anticipate the next shift and proactively address it before the CEO announces a new top priority.

## Actionable advice

1. **Start a weekly "state of the roadmap" update to the CEO.** Borrowing from Lenny's "state of Lenny" format discussed in the episode, send a short weekly update with three sections: (a) current top priorities and their status, (b) trade-offs and blockers that need CEO input, and (c) upcoming decisions or risks. This builds a communication rhythm that makes the CEO feel informed and reduces the impulse to override with new priorities -- because they can see what is already in flight and why it matters.

2. **When the CEO introduces a new priority, respond with trade-offs, not resistance.** Use Wes's framework inspired by her colleague Alex Peck: "Yes, we can prioritize this. That means X and Y will be delayed by Z weeks. Does that sound right, or would you prefer we keep the current plan?" This positions Andrew as a thoughtful operator, not a blocker. It also forces the CEO to confront the real cost of changing direction.

3. **Lead with the recommendation, then provide context.** When presenting roadmap updates, strategy proposals, or pushback on a priority shift, put the TLDR and recommendation at the top. Then add a "Context" section with rationale. This respects the CEO's attention and makes it easy for them to either approve quickly or dig deeper. Do not bury the ask inside paragraphs of background.

4. **Identify the pattern behind the CEO's shifting priorities.** Use the "eyes light up" principle to study what actually drives the CEO's enthusiasm. Track the last several priority shifts and look for a theme -- competitive anxiety, board feedback, a specific metric they are watching. Once the pattern is clear, Andrew can proactively address that underlying concern within the existing roadmap, reducing the chance the CEO feels the need to blow things up.

5. **Frame stability as a team performance issue, not a strategy debate.** Instead of arguing that the roadmap should not change (which can sound like resistance), talk about the impact on execution quality and team output. Wes's emphasis on trade-offs applies here: "When we shift priorities mid-quarter, it adds roughly X weeks of ramp-up time and we lose momentum on Y. Here is a way we can address the new priority while protecting our top two commitments." Make it about outcomes, not about who is right.

## Relevant experience

- **The Alex Peck trade-off method.** Wes described how her colleague Alex Peck at altMBA handled incoming requests without ever directly saying no. When Wes would throw new design requests over the wall, Alex would respond by naming the trade-off: "I can do this PDF today, which means the site redesign moves to later this week. Which would you prefer?" Wes noted that this approach made her feel in control and helped them both ensure the right work got done -- and it shifted the conversation from "are you a team player or not" to "how do we prioritize the right things." This is the exact model Andrew should use when the CEO drops a new priority on the team.

- **Avoiding surprises through proactive communication.** Wes emphasized that in the work context, surprises are almost always bad. She and Lenny discussed how the most effective senior people manage up by keeping their bosses informed so that leadership is never caught off guard -- and never feels the need to intervene with sudden changes. The implication for Andrew: if the CEO keeps introducing surprise priorities, it may be because the CEO does not feel sufficiently informed about what the team is doing and why it matters.

- **Senior people are the best at managing up.** Wes explicitly pushed back on the idea that managing up is something only junior people need to do. She argued the opposite -- that the most senior, most promoted people are the ones who do this best, because they understand what keeps their bosses up at night and take those things off their plate. For Andrew, this means treating the CEO's priority-shifting as a signal to manage up more effectively, not as something to simply endure.
