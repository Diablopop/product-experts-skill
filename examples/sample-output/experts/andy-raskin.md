# Advice from Andy Raskin

## Expert Background
Andy Raskin is a strategic narrative consultant who helps CEOs and company leaders align their teams around a single compelling story -- what he calls a "strategic narrative" -- that drives success across sales, marketing, product, fundraising, and recruiting. He has worked with leaders at Gong, Salesforce, Dropbox, Uber, and many other high-growth companies.

## Response to Your Question
> I'm a PM at a growth-stage startup. Every quarter, our CEO introduces a new "top priority," and the roadmap shifts accordingly. Morale is dropping and teams don't believe in long-term strategy anymore. How do I create strategic stability and credibility when leadership keeps changing direction?

## Key Insights

1. **The CEO's shifting priorities are a symptom of a missing strategic narrative, not a personality flaw.** Raskin's entire body of work centers on the idea that companies without a single unifying story default to reacting to every new feature request, competitive threat, or market signal. Without a narrative acting as a north star, every quarter feels like a reset because there is no durable frame to evaluate priorities against.

2. **A strategic narrative creates a decision-making bar for the product roadmap.** Raskin describes how CEOs he works with use the narrative as a filter: does this feature or initiative advance the movement, or not? Without that filter, the CEO has no principled way to say no -- so everything becomes the new top priority. The problem is not that the CEO changes direction; it is that there is no direction stable enough to change from.

3. **The narrative must come from the CEO, but a PM can be the catalyst.** Raskin is explicit that the most successful strategic narratives are CEO-led. However, he also notes that product leaders are frequently part of the "strategic narrative team" and that the narrative becomes the strategic north star for product. A PM who understands this framework is well-positioned to advocate for it.

4. **Alignment is not about agreement on tactics -- it is about shared belief in the movement.** When Raskin talks about Zuora, Gong, or 360Learning, the alignment those companies achieved was not about everyone agreeing on which features to build. It was about everyone -- from sales to engineering to marketing -- understanding and believing in the same shift-based story. Tactical disagreements become productive when there is strategic consensus.

## Actionable Advice

- **Frame the problem to the CEO in terms Raskin would use.** Do not walk in and say "we need to stop changing priorities." Instead, present the observation that the company lacks a single story that acts as a decision-making filter for the roadmap. Raskin's CEOs came to him because "they were constantly getting feature requests through sales, through customer success, and had no bar to decide what to take on and what not to take on." That is exactly the dynamic Andrew is describing. Name the absence.

- **Propose that the leadership team develop a strategic narrative using Raskin's five-piece structure:**
  1. Name the shift from the old game to the new game (e.g., "from X to Y").
  2. Name the stakes -- show who is winning by playing the new game and what happens to those who do not adapt.
  3. Name the object of the new game -- the rallying cry or mission that flows from the shift (ideally framed as a question: "What would it take to...?").
  4. Identify the obstacles to winning the new game.
  5. Show how the company's product helps overcome those obstacles.

  Once this exists, every quarterly "priority" can be evaluated against it. Does it advance the movement? If yes, prioritize. If no, deprioritize. The narrative becomes the stability mechanism.

- **Start small and test it.** Raskin advocates a lean approach. Even before the CEO buys in formally, Andrew can start testing a narrative hypothesis in his own team's work. Frame upcoming roadmap decisions through a draft version of the shift story. When pitching features internally, lead with the movement, not the feature. See if it creates more coherent conversations.

- **Interview customers about how they see the shift.** Raskin uses customer interviews as a core part of his process. When working with CEOs, he has the team "interview customers about how they see this shift, and sometimes the customers will literally give us the words." This is something Andrew can do immediately as a PM. Bring back customer language about the broader change happening in the market and use it to ground the narrative in evidence, not opinion.

- **Accept that the first draft will be wrong -- and that is the point.** Raskin is candid that the second session in his process is always painful. The first version of the narrative will be incomplete. But "having a shit draft is a million times more valuable than having all these great ideas." Do not wait for perfection. Get something concrete in front of leadership and iterate.

## Relevant Experience

Raskin tells the story of **Gong's CEO Amit Bendov**, who faced a version of this exact problem. Before establishing their strategic narrative ("from opinions to reality"), Gong was getting pulled in many directions by feature requests. After the narrative was in place, Bendov had a clear filter: "We get a lot of requests for features and a lot of them are basically about opinions, some way to record opinions. We're not going to do those." The narrative gave him the authority and clarity to say no, which stabilized the product direction.

Similarly, the **CEO Raskin interviewed the morning of the podcast recording** described the same dynamic: "We are constantly getting feature requests through sales, through customer success. And we had sort of no way, no bar to decide what do we take on, what don't we take on. And this [the strategic narrative] clearly has become our bar." This is exactly the tool Andrew's CEO is missing.

**360Learning** provides another relevant example. Before their strategic narrative, the CEO Nick Hernandez was pitching "collaborative learning" and constantly getting dragged into feature comparison conversations. After defining the shift from "top-down learning" to "upskill from within," the entire company had a stable frame. Nick reported that he stopped getting the old comparison questions entirely -- the narrative preempted them.

## Related Topics This Expert Covered
- **The five-piece strategic narrative framework** -- a structure for defining a company's story that replaces the traditional "problem-solution" pitch with a movement-based narrative (old game to new game, stakes, object of the game, obstacles, magic gifts)
- **Why category creation is insufficient without a story behind it** -- Raskin argues that a two-word category name means nothing without the underlying shift narrative; Gong's CEO said they "probably could have called it strawberry intelligence" because the story did the heavy lifting
- **When companies need a strategic narrative most** -- typically around Series B when the founders can no longer be in every room, or when the company is expanding into new products and the old story is no longer big enough
- **Testing the narrative with customers** -- using customer interviews and live sales calls to validate whether the shift story resonates before rolling it out broadly
